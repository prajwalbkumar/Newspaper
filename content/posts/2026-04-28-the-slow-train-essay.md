<span class="dateline">VIENNA —</span> The overnight train from Zurich arrives at Vienna Hauptbahnhof at 9:06am. I know this because I was on it, awake at 6:30 when the Alps began to flatten into the wide Danube plain, watching the pale April light catch the last of the snowfields above Salzburg. I had a small compartment, a bunk, a window I could actually open. By the time the train arrived, I had been inside the journey long enough to belong to it. Vienna did not ambush me.

This matters more than I expected it to.

## What Flying Does to Arrival

I have landed in Dubai on hundreds of flights. I have experienced Dubai many times, but I have never quite *arrived* in Dubai. The airport is a pressurised container that could be in any city in any country. The taxi is another container. The apartment or hotel is a third. The city appears, fully formed and disorienting, somewhere around the third container, when the containers finally stop.

The brain requires geography to orient itself. Not the conceptual geography of maps and distances — the felt geography of passing through terrain, watching the landscape shift, feeling the temperature change, noticing what the buildings look like as the density increases from countryside to suburb to city. This is what you surrender when you fly. The journey is not travel. It is transport.

The train between Vienna and Zurich takes approximately eight hours depending on routing. By air, the same journey takes one hour and fifteen minutes, plus check-in, plus security, plus the forty-five minutes you spend in the airport before your gate is announced, plus the taxi on either end, plus the psychic cost of the transition. The net time difference is roughly ninety minutes. The difference in what you arrive with is [something I find difficult to quantify but impossible to dismiss]{redact}.

## The Attention Economy of Moving Slowly

On a train, there is nothing to do except look out of the window, read, think, or talk to the person next to you. There is no turbulence. There are no seatbelt signs. The wifi is patchy enough that working is usually not worth the effort. The train enforces a kind of attention that the modern world has almost entirely eliminated: attention with no deliverable.

I brought three books on the Vienna trip. I finished one. I started another. I spent two hours watching Switzerland pass — specifically the section between Buchs and Bregenz where the Rhine runs alongside the track and the mountains on the Austrian side are close enough that you can see the rock faces. I thought about nothing in particular. I was not productive. I arrived refreshed in a way that airports have never produced in me.

This is not a romantic's nostalgia for a mode of transport. It is an argument about cognitive cost. Every transition — airport to gate to aircraft to gate to taxi — is a micro-stressor. The cumulative cognitive load of a short-haul flight is higher than most people account for, because the stressors are individually small enough to be dismissed. The body keeps the score on these things. The persistent mid-afternoon tiredness after a "short" flight is the score being kept.

## What the Track Teaches You About the Country

The Paris–Barcelona overnight. The Edinburgh–London day train through the Pennines. The Munich–Rome through the Brenner Pass. The Tokyo–Osaka Shinkansen, which is fast enough to be something else entirely — not slow travel, but a demonstration of what infrastructure looks like when a country takes trains seriously. Each of these journeys contains information about the country that no airport communicates.

From a train, you see the industrial zones, the suburban interfaces, the back of things. Airports show you cities at their most curated face — the new terminals, the motorway approach. Trains show you the urban archaeology: the old factories, the allotments, the unglamorous parts of cities that people actually live in. The approach to London by train from the north shows you, in about forty minutes, a complete history of post-war British planning. No flight could do this.

I learned more about Switzerland in the eight hours between Zurich and Vienna than in two previous visits by plane. The neatness of the farms. The impeccable condition of the infrastructure — every bridge, every retaining wall, every rural station rendered as if maintenance matters. The wealth visible in the texture of ordinary things, not in any single extravagant object. From the air, Switzerland is a map. From the train, it is a place.

## The Carbon Arithmetic

The environmental argument for trains is well-documented and I do not want to rehearse it at length. The short version: a flight from Zurich to Vienna produces approximately 200 times more CO₂ per passenger-kilometre than the equivalent rail journey. The longer version involves offsets, airline efficiency improvements, and the complicated moral accounting of [a person who flew to get to Zurich in the first place]{redact}. The shorter version is that the environmental cost of flying is not a future problem. It is a present one.

The slower route has the lower footprint. This is not always achievable and the point of this essay is not guilt. The point is that when the slow route is available and the time cost is marginal, choosing the fast route is a choice, not a necessity.

## On Arriving Having Earned It

There is a quality to arriving by train that I can only describe as *having earned it*. Not in the punishing sense — not suffering for the destination — but in the sense of having paid attention for the duration. The journey was part of the experience, not an inconvenience before the experience. Vienna was not an ambush. It was the end of a continuous thread that began in Zurich.

I checked into the hotel at 9:45am, dropped my bag, and walked out into the city. The April light was the same light I had been watching through the train window for three hours. The temperature was roughly what the topography of the final approach had suggested. The city connected to the journey. I was oriented.

I booked the return by train before I had dinner.

## A Practical Note

For the regular objection — the cost — the overnight train between Zurich and Vienna in a private sleeper coupé costs approximately the same as a budget flight plus taxi on each end plus one night's accommodation, because you sleep on the train. The comparison is rarely made correctly because people price the ticket in isolation and don't subtract the hotel night. The overnight train replaces both a flight and a night in a hotel. On this accounting, it is frequently cheaper than flying.

The second objection is inflexibility: trains have fixed routes, limited frequencies, routes that don't connect everywhere. This is correct. [There are journeys I fly because there is no reasonable alternative]{redact}. Dubai to Tokyo. Dubai to New York. Long-haul intercontinental routes where the train option requires a week and several ferries. I am not arguing for a hairshirt approach to travel.

I am arguing for choosing the slow route when it exists and the cost is manageable. The return on that investment is not measured in destination efficiency. It is measured in arriving somewhere as a person who has been travelling, rather than a person who has been transported.

The 9:06am arrival at Vienna Hauptbahnhof was one of the better travel experiences of recent memory. The 11:15am arrival by air at Vienna Airport was also, once, a travel experience. I do not remember it.
