## Opening: Set the Scene

The opening section establishes what this is about and why it matters. Don't bury the lede. A long read earns its length by going deeper, not by covering more surface area.

First paragraph. Get into the argument quickly. The reader chose to click "Read in full" — reward that immediately.

Second paragraph continues the opening thought. Use the first section to orient the reader: here's the thing, here's why it matters, here's what you'll find out.

## Section Two: The Evidence

A long read is built from sections. Each section should do one thing. Name the section with the thing it does, not with a clever title.

Prose in this section. A long read can be as long as needed — the popup scrolls, and the standalone page is built for reading. Don't pad. Every paragraph should earn its place.

Use `[text]{redact}` for passages you're not ready to publish in full but want to preserve in the document.

> A blockquote. Use these to isolate a key insight, a piece of research, or a quote that deserves to stand alone. They render with an accent-colored left border.

### A Sub-Section Heading

Use `###` for sub-sections within a major section. They render in italic serif — heavier than body text, lighter than the H2 section heading.

More prose. Tables, code blocks, images, and callouts all work here. Use them when they genuinely help the argument. Don't use them for decoration.

## Section Three: The Conclusion

The conclusion should land, not summarize. You've made the argument — now what does it mean? What should the reader do, think, or notice differently?

Final paragraph. A long read should end with enough force that the reader feels the ending rather than just noticing the text has stopped.
